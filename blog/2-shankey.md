### The Problem with Spreadsheets

When new clients come to me, they usually bring a spreadsheet. It has 500 rows of data, neat columns, and perfect totals.

The problem? **The human brain is bad at reading rows.**

A spreadsheet can tell you that you spent ₹50,000 on credit cards. But it cannot show you the *relationship* between your earnings, your transfers, and your ultimate spending. That is why, as a Data Consultant, I convert those boring rows into **Cash Flow Maps**.

Here is why this visual approach changes how my clients see their money.

### 1. Visualizing "Proportion" (The Big Picture)

Most people know their salary, and they know their rent. But they lose track of everything in the middle.

I use **Sankey Diagrams** to show the "weight" of money. In a spreadsheet, a ₹10,000 expense looks just like a ₹1,00,000 expense—it's just a number in a cell. But in a visualization, the *size* of the bar matches the amount of money.

![Sankey diagram showing broad categories of spending](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/rikithreddy/rikithreddy.github.io/master/public/assets/2-shankey/img1.png)

**Why this is needed:**
In one second, you can see the truth. You don't need to do math. If the red "Lifestyle" block is five times bigger than the green "Investments" block, you immediately know your priorities are off. You can't hide from the visual size of your spending.

### 2. Uncovering "Structural Complexity"

Money doesn't just sit in one place; it moves. It flows from salary accounts to savings, then to wallets, then to credit cards.

When I map this journey for clients, we often find a tangled mess.

![Complex flow diagram showing money moving between banks and cards](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/rikithreddy/rikithreddy.github.io/master/public/assets/2-shankey/img2.png)

**Why this is needed:**
This type of map reveals **inefficiency**. When you see lines crossing over each other—moving money from Bank A to Bank B to Credit Card C—you realize how complicated you have made your financial life. Complexity is where leaks happen. This visualization forces us to ask: *"Why do we have three steps just to pay for dinner?"*

### 3. Opening the "Black Box"

The biggest lie in personal finance is the line item: **"Credit Card Bill."**

In a standard expense report, "Credit Card Payment" is listed as a single category. This is dangerous because it hides your actual habits. I use visualizations to "explode" that single block into its actual components.

![Detailed breakdown of credit card spending into merchants](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/rikithreddy/rikithreddy.github.io/master/public/assets/2-shankey/img3.png)

**Why this is needed:**
This turns a "bill" into "behavior." Instead of seeing a generic payment, you see the granular truth: *Uber, Zomato, Amazon, Breweries.* It strips away the mask of the credit card and shows you exactly who is taking your money.

### The Consultant's Philosophy

I don't make these charts just to make the report look pretty. I make them because **you cannot fix what you cannot see.**

Rows and columns hide your habits. Visualizations expose them. Once you see the flow, fixing the leak becomes obvious.
